The genetics of eye color: why brown appears dominant

Melanin is the pigment that determines brown eyes, while the lack of it causes blue eyes through a scattering effect. Think of melanin as the iris' built-in light dimmer—the more pigment you have, the richer and darker the color.

Blue and green eyes do not contain blue pigments. Instead, they rely on Rayleigh scattering, the same phenomenon that makes the sky look blue. Light enters the iris, bounces through structural layers, and reflects back out in cooler tones based on the remaining trace pigments.

Up to sixteen genes take part in this chromatic symphony, but OCA2 and HERC2 steal the spotlight. HERC2 is the switch that determines whether OCA2 gets to produce melanin. A tiny mutation—essentially an off switch—reduces pigment production and reveals blue hues.

When translating complex science into marketing stories, I lean on this narrative arc: explain the phenomenon, humanize the mechanism, and end with a memorable metaphor. Facts stay intact, but curiosity leads the way.
